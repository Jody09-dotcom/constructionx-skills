# Example Outputs

Five worked examples produced by the toolbox-talk-generator skill across the most common UK site hazards. These are not templates to fill in. They are reference outputs showing the shape, tone, and level of specificity the skill aims for.

Each example was generated from a one-line risk topic. The site name, foreman name, and date are placeholders.

---

## Example 1: Working at height

**Input:** "Working at height"

```markdown
# Toolbox Talk: Working at Height

**Site:** {Site}
**Date:** {YYYY-MM-DD}
**Foreman:** {Name}
**Trades briefed:** All trades on site
**Read time:** 5 minutes

---

## Why we are talking about this today

Falls from height kill more construction workers in the UK every year than any other cause. About 35 to 40 deaths a year. Most of them from less than 4 metres.

Anyone working off the ground today, even off a hop-up or a stepladder, is exposed. We have got scaffold up on the east elevation and roofers due tomorrow. Today is the day to walk the work and look at it before someone else does it under pressure.

---

## Key points

**1. Plan the access before you do the work, not while you are doing it.**
Last year a sparky in Lincolnshire stood a stepladder on a stack of insulation boards to reach a downlighter. The stack shifted, he came down with the ladder, broke his wrist, lost three weeks. The downlighter could have waited an hour for a tower to be wheeled in. Always pick the access first, then the task.

**2. Check the equipment every single time you use it.**
Tags rip off, ladders get banged, scaffold gets clipped by deliveries overnight. Before you climb, look at it. Feet flat on the ground, no twisted stiles, tag in date, tower wheels locked. Five seconds of looking saves a day at A&E.

**3. Three points of contact on a ladder, every move.**
Two hands one foot, or two feet one hand. If you cannot keep three points of contact, you should not be on a ladder, you should be on a platform. Carrying tools up a ladder by hand is the classic way to lose a grip.

**4. Below is as dangerous as above.**
A spanner dropped from 3 metres has the same effect on a hard hat as a hammer thrown from your shoulder. If anyone is working overhead, the area underneath is a no-go. Brush-off zones, kickboards, tool tethers. If you are below, look up before you walk.

---

## Question for the team

Where on this site this week could a ladder slide out from under you, and what would you change about the setup right now?

---

## Do this today

Before any ladder, hop-up, or tower goes into use today, walk to the foot of it and check three things: the ground (no soft spots, no debris, no spilled water), the tag (in date, no damage), and the surroundings (nothing being lifted overhead, nobody crossing underneath). If any of the three is wrong, do not use it. Find the foreman.

---

## HSE guidance

INDG143, Working at height: A brief guide. hse.gov.uk/pubns/indg143.pdf

Working at Height Regulations 2005 is the underlying law. The HSE construction pages are the working reference: hse.gov.uk/construction/
```

## Example 2: Manual handling

**Input:** "Manual handling"

```markdown
# Toolbox Talk: Manual Handling

**Site:** {Site}
**Date:** {YYYY-MM-DD}
**Foreman:** {Name}
**Trades briefed:** All trades on site
**Read time:** 5 minutes

---

## Why we are talking about this today

Manual handling is the biggest cause of reported injury on UK construction sites. Not falls. Not vehicles. Lifting things. About a third of over-7-day injuries that get reported under RIDDOR every year.

We have got plasterboards, kerbs, and bagged materials moving on this job all week. The stuff that puts a back out is the stuff that nobody thinks twice about.

---

## Key points

**1. The first question is "do I have to lift it at all?".**
Pallet truck, sack barrow, telehandler, two-man lift. Last summer in Norwich a bricklayer's mate tried to carry a 25kg bag of plaster from the stack to the mixer because the wheelbarrow was thirty feet away. Bag slipped, he lifted it back from a bad position, and the strain pulled it out of his grip. Three weeks signed off. The wheelbarrow was always the right answer.

**2. Lift from the legs, not the back, every time.**
Feet shoulder-width, knees bent, back straight, load close to the body. The tired version of you at four o'clock is the one that bends from the waist because it is quicker. Do not be the tired version of you.

**3. Two-person lifts have a leader.**
If you are lifting in a pair and you do not know who is calling it, you will drop it. One person calls "ready, lift, walk, down". Agree it before you bend down, not while you are holding the load.

**4. Bagged materials, kerbs, and plasterboard are the danger list.**
Anything around 25kg, awkward shape, sealed surface that slips out of grip when sweaty. Treat them as a two-person job by default. The lone hero lift is how a 30-year-old ends up with a 60-year-old's lower back.

---

## Question for the team

What is the heaviest thing you have lifted on your own this week, and what could you have used instead?

---

## Do this today

Walk the site once before first break and identify every load over 20kg that is going to move today. Tag the ones that need a barrow, a teleporter, or a two-man lift, and tell the gang foreman before lunchtime. No solo hero lifts on this job.

---

## HSE guidance

L23, Manual Handling Operations Regulations 1992 (as amended): guidance on the regulations. hse.gov.uk/pubns/books/l23.htm

Short version: INDG143 (manual handling at work) and the HSE manual handling assessment charts (MAC tool) at hse.gov.uk/msd/mac/
```

## Example 3: Wet weather working

**Input:** "Wet weather working"

```markdown
# Toolbox Talk: Wet Weather Working

**Site:** {Site}
**Date:** {YYYY-MM-DD}
**Foreman:** {Name}
**Trades briefed:** All trades on site
**Read time:** 5 minutes

---

## Why we are talking about this today

Forecast is heavy rain through the afternoon. Wet weather changes the site. Surfaces that were grippy at eight in the morning are slippy by ten. Excavations fill. Scaffold boards go from solid to greasy. Power tools and water do not mix.

Most wet-weather incidents are not dramatic. They are the slip on a metal kicker plate, the trip on a trailing lead in standing water, the boot full of wet concrete that ends a day early.

---

## Key points

**1. Walk the access routes before the rain hits, not after.**
If there is anything on the path that becomes a slip risk when wet (steel plates, painted scaffold boards, cut-offs, plastic sheeting, mud that turns to slurry), move it or cone it now. The thirty seconds before the rain saves the thirty minutes after.

**2. Excavations fill. Sides soften.**
Any open excavation needs a look before someone goes back in after rain. The sides that were dry yesterday are sucking water today. If it has rained more than twenty minutes, treat the excavation as new. Get the foreman to inspect before re-entry.

**3. Electric tools and water are a hospital trip.**
110V is safer than 240V but still not safe in standing water. If a lead is in a puddle, it is a fault waiting to happen. Lift leads off the floor. Use hooks, not the deck. RCD on every site supply, no exceptions.

**4. Hi-vis gets dimmer when it is soaked.**
Wet hi-vis reflects worse than dry hi-vis. Plant operators see less in the rain. If you are working near plant or vehicles, double check eye contact with the driver before you move. Banksman if in doubt.

---

## Question for the team

Where on this site, today, will the surface get most slippy first when the rain starts, and who is the one most likely to be standing on it?

---

## Do this today

Before lunch, walk the main access from the gate to the welfare cabin and back, and identify every metal plate, painted surface, or sheet of polythene on the route. Get them lifted, gritted, or coned by the time the rain hits. Tell the foreman if anything cannot be moved before the weather turns.

---

## HSE guidance

HSG150, Health and safety in construction (general construction H&S). hse.gov.uk/pubns/books/hsg150.htm

INDG401 covers slips and trips: hse.gov.uk/pubns/indg401.pdf

For wet-weather-specific scaffold inspection, refer to TG20 and the daily scaffold inspection regime under SG4 (NASC guidance, in addition to HSE).
```

## Example 4: Lone working on site

**Input:** "Lone working"

```markdown
# Toolbox Talk: Lone Working on Site

**Site:** {Site}
**Date:** {YYYY-MM-DD}
**Foreman:** {Name}
**Trades briefed:** All trades, particularly anyone first in or last out
**Read time:** 5 minutes

---

## Why we are talking about this today

Most days on this site there are at least four of us on the deck. But the first hour and the last hour have one person on their own. Boiler service, snagging visits, lock-up at half five. A trip, a stroke, a power tool kickback when you are alone, and the next person to find you is the person opening the gate the next morning.

Lone working is not banned. It needs to be planned.

---

## Key points

**1. If you are working alone, somebody knows you are working alone.**
Foreman, partner at home, office. They know where you are, what you are doing, and what time you are out. The check-out call when you leave is not optional. No call, the foreman comes looking.

**2. High-risk works do not happen alone, full stop.**
Working at height above 2 metres, hot works, confined space, live electrical, lifting operations, work near live traffic. If the RAMS calls for two operatives, it calls for two operatives. The hour you save is not worth the life it costs.

**3. Phone signal is part of the risk assessment.**
If the cellar of the building has no signal, the lone worker procedure does not work in the cellar. Either fix the signal (booster, walkie-talkie, set check-in interval) or do not work alone in the cellar.

**4. The check-in interval is shorter for higher risk.**
Routine office or above-ground work, hourly check-in is enough. Anything with a tool in your hand, every thirty minutes. If the check-in is missed, the foreman or office calls. If the call fails, the foreman drives. There is no scenario where a missed check-in is left until tomorrow.

---

## Question for the team

If you collapsed at the back of the site right now, how long would it be before someone realised, and would they know where to find you?

---

## Do this today

If you are first in or last out today, message the foreman at the start and end of your stint with your location and what you are working on. If you do not have signal in part of the site, walk back out before the end of the stint to send the all-clear. No exceptions, even if it is "just locking up".

---

## HSE guidance

INDG73, Working alone: Health and safety guidance on the risks of lone working. hse.gov.uk/pubns/indg73.pdf

The risk assessment for lone working sits alongside the site's general risk assessment under the Management of Health and Safety at Work Regulations 1999.
```

## Example 5: Asbestos awareness

**Input:** "Asbestos awareness"

```markdown
# Toolbox Talk: Asbestos Awareness

**Site:** {Site}
**Date:** {YYYY-MM-DD}
**Foreman:** {Name}
**Trades briefed:** All trades on site, particularly carpenters, sparkies, plumbers, plasterers
**Read time:** 5 minutes

---

## Why we are talking about this today

Around 5,000 people a year still die in the UK from asbestos-related disease. Most of them tradesmen, exposed decades ago, before anyone knew. Anyone working on a building put up before 2000 is in scope. We are working in a 1972 property this week. Asbestos is the live risk on this job.

This is not about scaring anyone. It is about making sure nobody on this site eats their lunch with a hand that has been on the wrong panel.

---

## Key points

**1. If you do not know what it is, do not touch it.**
Old textured ceilings (Artex up to about 1999), pipe lagging, AIB ceiling tiles, soffits, garage roof sheets, vinyl floor tiles. If it looks like it might be asbestos and there is no asbestos register on file, stop. Tell the foreman. Do not drill it, cut it, sand it, or break it.

**2. The asbestos survey lives on site.**
Refurbishment and Demolition surveys are the ones that matter for our work. There is one for this property, foreman has it. Before any invasive work, read the relevant section. Five minutes with a survey beats forty years of looking over your shoulder.

**3. Power tools and asbestos do not mix, ever.**
Hand tools only when working near anything suspect. The standard tradesman exposure is the sparky drilling through a cellar wall, the plumber cutting a pipe lagged in 1968, the plasterer sanding "just a bit" of Artex. The dust is the killer. Power tools generate dust. Hand tools and damp methods only.

**4. If you think you have disturbed asbestos, stop and seal the area.**
Do not try to clean it up. Do not vacuum it. Do not sweep. Stop work. Get everyone out. Foreman calls the licensed asbestos contractor. Anyone in the immediate area gets their clothes bagged, not washed at home.

---

## Question for the team

If you walked into a room on this site right now and saw a panel you were not sure about, who would you tell, and what would you do until someone got there?

---

## Do this today

Before any drilling, cutting, sanding, or fixing into the existing fabric of this property today, ask the foreman to show you the asbestos survey for that location. If the location is not on the survey, or the survey is unclear, stop and wait. The job will not move until that question is answered.

---

## HSE guidance

INDG223, A short guide to managing asbestos in premises. hse.gov.uk/pubns/indg223.pdf

L143, Managing and working with asbestos (the ACoP). hse.gov.uk/pubns/books/l143.htm

For licensed asbestos work, the Control of Asbestos Regulations 2012 applies. We do NOT do licensed work. Anything licensed gets handed to a licensed contractor.
```
