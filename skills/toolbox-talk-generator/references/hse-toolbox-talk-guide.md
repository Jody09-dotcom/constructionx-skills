# HSE Toolbox Talk Guide

Background reference for the toolbox-talk-generator skill. Covers what HSE expects from a toolbox talk, common hazard categories with the relevant document numbers, and record-keeping practice.

## What HSE expects from a toolbox talk

HSE does not prescribe a fixed format. The expectation is:

1. The talk addresses a specific, current hazard relevant to the work being done.
2. The talk is delivered before the work it relates to (or at the start of the working week for general hazards).
3. The people briefed sign or otherwise acknowledge the briefing.
4. The record is kept and available for inspection.

The principal contractor's Construction Phase Plan (CPP) under CDM 2015 typically references toolbox talks as part of the day-to-day H&S management. Insurers and clients increasingly ask to see the records as part of their due diligence.

## Frequency

Industry standard for UK construction sites is one toolbox talk per week per gang. Higher-risk works (work at height, lifting, confined space, hot works, asbestos) get a pre-task briefing in addition to the weekly talk.

## Record-keeping

The signed toolbox talk lives in the site file (paper or digital) for the duration of the project, plus three years post-completion as standard practice. For construction projects with a longer-tail risk profile (asbestos, structural works that might be revisited), keep records longer. Consult the CDM 2015 file requirements.

## Cross-references

- A toolbox talk does NOT replace a RAMS. A RAMS authorises work; a toolbox talk reminds operatives of the controls in the RAMS.
- A toolbox talk does NOT replace a site induction. Induction covers the site as a whole; toolbox talks cover a specific hazard.
- A toolbox talk does NOT replace RIDDOR reporting. RIDDOR-reportable incidents go to HSE separately at hse.gov.uk/riddor.

## Common pitfalls

- Generic talks that could apply to any site any week. Tie the talk to today's work, today's weather, this week's near-miss.
- Talks that read like a lecture. Aim for five minutes spoken aloud. Three to five points beats fifteen.
- Talks delivered after the work has started. Brief before, not after.
- Talks signed off without being read. The foreman is responsible. AI drafts a starting point; the foreman owns the delivery.
- Confabulated regulations. If the document number isn't certain, the talk says so. Wrong references get a site manager into more trouble than no reference at all.
